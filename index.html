Open Source Open Data

Manifesto

We believe in the power of open data to transform society, and that data is only truly open if it is collected, processed, and shared using free and open source tools.

Our efforts are driven by two principles:
  1. Information produced by publicly-funded activity belongs to the people.
  2. Open data is only open if the tools used to process and share it are also open.

That means:

  • Data collected with public resources by public institutions should be free and open to the public
  • Data is only truly free and open if it is collected, processed, and distributed in a way that it is free from any proprietary tools or ecosystems that limit its use and sharing
  • The software used to collect, modify, share or visualize public data should be free and open to the public
  • Public institutions should collaborate and reuse each others’ software
  • Vendors and others serving government entities should add value by facilitating this collaboration and reuse, rather than extracting value through restrictive licensing
  • Processes for collecting, managing, and sharing data should be transparent

What is open data?

Open data is data that can be freely used, modified, and shared by anyone for any purpose. Our public institutions collect all kinds of data - health data, environmental data, crime data, and much more. These institutions are often mandated or simply choose to share this data with the public. Openness is achieved through availability, discoverability, open licensing, machine readability, and standard formatting.

Why is data such an important asset?

In an increasingly digital society, data gives us opportunities to make a real difference in our communities. Our governments can use data to drive decisions about our lives; the citizenry can use data to hold their officials and institutions accountable; civic groups can create targeted initiatives to improve their communities; and much more. With the data we need, we become empowered to truly understand and interpret our world and to drive meaningful, positive change.

What is open source?

Free and open-source software is software whose code can be accessed, modified, and shared by anyone. Open-source software offers complete transparency into the code and technology used to support an organization’s work. It means that the software can be extended to the specific needs of an organization, and can be reused by anyone who needs it. Open-source solutions are free, extensible, and will never be tied to a single vendor.

Why are the tools around open data important?

Open data belongs to the public, and that means the public should have full access to it and full transparency into the tools public institutions use to manage that data. All the principles that apply to open data should also apply to the tools that collect, process, publish, and analyze it. When we hold our tools to high standards of transparency and accessibility, our data becomes more free.

Benefits

Benefist of Open Source Open Data tools include:

  • Technical control over all internal open data efforts
  • Freedom to fully engage with the open data community at large
  • No vendor lock-in
  • Complete control over the source code
  • Complete control of data
  • Complete control of the user’s profile data
  • Ability to customize tools for local use cases
  • Transparency
  • Re-use
  • Reduced Cost of ownership
